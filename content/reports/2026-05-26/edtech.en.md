## Why it matters (reasoning)
The signal underneath the noise: the mass-market gamified-language category (Duolingo archetype) is losing narrative control to (a) general-purpose LLMs used as 1:1 tutors [29], and (b) niche/L1-anchored apps [43]. That's a classic disruption pattern — the incumbent's moat (streaks, gamification) is being reframed as the product's flaw [36][37]. For K-12, the teacher-side posts [1][3] show the buyer's actual pain is behavior, attendance, and parent dynamics — not lack of content. Tools that don't reduce teacher workload will keep failing procurement. Pronunciation's virality [5][6][30][44] reveals an underserved wedge: most apps still treat pronunciation as a checkbox, not a core loop, even though it's the most socially-charged language skill (accent = identity, embarrassment, fandom currency). Second-order: as LLM tutors absorb language-learning demand, content-only edtech valuations compress further [12]; differentiation shifts to speech, assessment, classroom workflow, and verticalized L1 pairs.
