Government-side, NSTDA and the Ministry of Education said they will apply AI research to reduce teacher document workload and manage student meals [2], while the Ministry of Labour framed workforce policy as needing to be 'practical' and tied to market demand [4]. The strongest signal is direction, not product: multiple actors (a major opposition party [1][5], NSTDA [2], AIT [13]) are converging on the same idea — Thailand should build narrow, domain-specific Thai-language AI rather than general frontier models. The caution flags ('must be transparent and worthwhile' on TH-AI Passport [7]) signal these are still early, politically-contested programs, not procurement-ready pipelines.
